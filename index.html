Schrodinger equation

We are trying to solve the Schrodinger equation for some simple cases. Schrodinger equation for a single particle wave function,\(\psi(\mathbf{r}, t)\) in a potential, \(V(\mathbf{r})\) is given as follows. \[ i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi(\mathbf{r}, t) =\hat{H}\psi(\mathbf{r}, t)= \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\mathbf{r}) \right]\psi(\mathbf{r}, t) \] In the quantum mechanics lecture, we understand this differential equation with steady state solutions or time-independent Schrodinger equation. We obtain the equation by using an ansatz that gives time independent probability distribution of the particle, \(\psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \psi(\mathbf{r})e^{-iEt/\hbar}\), where \(E\) is a constant called energy eigenvalue. Inserting to the Schrodinger equation, we get the following. \[ E\psi(\mathbf{r}) = \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\mathbf{r}) \right]\psi(\mathbf{r}) \] Depends on the functional form of the potential, one can solve or can not solve the equation by hand. First, we are going to solve this equation with COMSOL for the case we can solve exactly by hand. Then, we extend the potential to some interesting cases. We work with several potentials as a practice.

Time-independent Schrodinger equation - well

For 1D- infinite well of width \(L\), we have following form of the potential. \[ V = \begin{cases} \infty,& \text{if } |x|> L/2 \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \] The equation inside the well is simply the Helmholtz equation we solved before. \[ \frac{d^2}{dx^2}\psi(x) + \frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}\psi(x) = 0 \] The eigenvalues of this system is \[ E_n = \frac{\hbar^2 k_n^2}{2m}= \frac{\hbar^2 \pi^2 n^2}{2mL^2}, \quad n = 1,2,3, ... \] with corresponding eigenfunctions. \[ \psi_n (x)= \begin{cases} \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\cos(k_n x), & n = 1,3,5,... \\ \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin(k_n x), & n = 2,4,6,... \end{cases} \] One of the key point here is that the energy eigenvalue has quadratic dependence on the wave vector, \(k_n\) as for the classical free-space energy (\(E = p^2/2m=(\hbar k)^2/2m\)). We can imagine is that by increasing the system size, \(L\) to infinity than the eigenvalue becomes continuum (spacing between the eigenvalue vanishes). Now, we bravely call an eigenstate that has (almost) vanishing energy as a “unbound” state.

One can recall the spectrum of the hydrogen. The eigenenergy scales as \(-1/n^2\) and the spacing between the nearby energy level vanishes as \(n\) or the energy grows.

1D Simple harmonic oscillator (SHO)

Now we consider a simple harmonic oscillator of angular frequency, \(\omega\). The energy eigenvalue is \[ E_{n} = \hbar\omega(n+\frac{1}{2}), \quad n=0, 1, 2, ... \] SHO can be realize by inserting 1e4 [1/m^4]* x^2 in \(a\) We see that the eigenvalue is linearly scales with \(n\).

First three eigenstates are Hermite-gaussian functions.

Notice that one should use strong enough spring constant (so I put 1e4) to localize the wavefunction. If this is not strong enough, the boundary condition (infinite well) is more dominant than the harmonic potential, resulting in more like a previous case.

Time-dependent Schrodinger equation

Steady state gives us many useful information, however, it does not tell us how a wave packet moves after launched to some velocity. We solve time dependent Schrodinger equation with a Gaussian shaped wave packet.

We can solve the dynamics with choosing time dependnent study and modify the coefficients to have Schrodinger equation. Now the variable, \(u\) is a complex number.

We are going to use a wavefunction of the following shape. (Forget about the normalization for a moment) \(\psi (x) = \exp\left[ -\frac{x^2}{4\sigma^2} \right]\)

First we try to find out how a Gaussian wavepacket without net momentum spreading in the well. At the end, it is reflected from the boundary of the well, (I impose Dirichelet Boundary) shattered into some uniform shape. Notice that it is easy for simulation to blow up since the spatial frequency becomes very high after the reflection.

One can calculate how does wavepacket spread over time without any potential (infinite space, no boundary like the simulation). \[ \sigma(t) = \sigma(0) \sqrt{1+ \left(\frac{t}{\tau}\right)^2}, \quad \text{where } \tau = 2m\sigma^2/\hbar \] Notice that the it takes more time to spread when the mass or the initial uncertainty (\(\sigma\)) is larger.

If there is a harmonic potential, we expect it not to spread anymore. Let us impose a harmonic potential.

The width of the packet oscillates over time because it not close to the eigenstate. Here we showed that when the width of the initial packet is smaller than the eigenstate value. Following animation is when the initial state is larger than the eigenstate value. We see that the packet is compressed in the beginning.

If one get closer to the eigenvalue, it barely oscillates showing the meaning of the ‘steady state’.

Let us give a kick to the Gaussian packet. Giving a momentum kick can be done naively by just imposing a phase factor, \(e^{imvx}\) to the wavefunction. Give \(0\) for \(\partial u / \partial t\) initial condition for now. Note that this is not exact way to launch the Gaussian packet in free space but considering this as an attempt. In a well, it spreads while moving, reflects at the boundary.

With the harmonic potential, it oscillates over time while maintaining its width.
